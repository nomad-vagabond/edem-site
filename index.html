The smoothest moon

Europa is the second and the smallest of the four Galilean moons orbiting Jupiter. Being slightly smaller than Earth's moon, it orbits Jupiter every 3.5 days and is locked by gravity to Jupiter, so the the same hemisphere of the moon always faces the planet.

Europa has the smoothest surface of any known solid object in the Solar System, which consists of water ice and crisscrossed by long, linear fractures. Based on the small number of craters observed, the surface of this moon appears to be no more than 40 to 90 million years old, which is quite young in geologic terms.

The apparent youth and smoothness of the surface have led to the hypothesis that a water ocean exists beneath it, which could conceivably harbor extraterrestrial life. While evidence for this internal ocean is quite strong, its presence awaits confirmation by future missions. And the most challenging one would be an ice penetrating mission that will be targeted at taking water probes and reaching the ocean floor in search for possible life forms.

The ice shield

Europa is unique among the large icy satellites. Its geodynamically active and relatively thin (3…30 km thick) ice crust covers the global water ocean that may reach over 100 km in depth. Along with a liquid ocean, Europa also likely harbors a rocky seafloor that can supply energy and elements needed to drive metabolism.

Europa’s unusual surface is dominated by tectonic features in the form of linear ridges, bands, and fractures. The most striking of them are dark streaks crisscrossing the entire globe, called lineae. The most likely hypothesis states that the lineae on Europa may have been produced by a series of eruptions of warm ice as the crust spread open to expose warmer layers beneath. The signs of some suface activity that bay be water plumes have been detected recently by the Hubble telescope. But still there is no solid evidence that water from the inner ocean is able to reach the surface through cracks in the ice crust.

This leads to the necessity in considering different possible ways of reaching Europa inner ocean in the frame of future landing missions. While most existing concepts of the Europa's ice crust penetration are oriented towards the development of an ice drillig or melting autonomous vehicles, there exist other means. And the most direct one is the high energy kinetic impact, capable of leaving a deep socket in ice, that may trail down to the deep warm ice layers or even pierce the whole ice crust.

EDEM approach

Europa is the smoothest known object in the Solar System, lacking large-scale features such as mountains and craters. Europa's most striking surface features are a series of dark streaks crisscrossing the entire globe, called lineae. Other features present on Europa are circular and elliptical lenticulae.

Scientists' consensus is that a layer of liquid water exists beneath Europa's surface, and that heat from tidal flexing allows the subsurface ocean to remain liquid. It is estimated that the outer crust of solid ice is approximately 10–30 km. The liquid ocean underneath may be about 100 km.
